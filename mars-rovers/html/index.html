Robots on a Frontier World

NASA's Mars rovers are robotic scientists designed to investigate the Red Planet's geology, climate, and potential for past life. Built and operated by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL), these missions extend human curiosity millions of kilometers from Earth.

This site highlights two of NASA's most advanced rovers: Curiosity and Perseverance, with a nod to the trailblazing Sojourner.

Learn how these machines launch, land, drive, drill, and discover in some of the harshest conditions known.

Featured Mars Rovers

Perseverance Rover

Sample caching and the search for signs of ancient life.

Mission Goals

  • Search for signs of ancient microbial life in Jezero Crater.
  • Collect and cache rock and soil samples for eventual return to Earth.
  • Characterize Mars' climate and geology.
  • Test new technologies to prepare for future human exploration.

Launch and Landing

Perseverance launched on an Atlas V rocket from Cape Canaveral in July 2020 and successfully landed in Jezero Crater in February 2021 using a sky-crane descent system.

Key Technical Features

  • Advanced sample caching system with sealed titanium tubes.
  • Powerful science instruments for imaging, chemistry, and mineralogy.
  • Onboard microphone and high-resolution cameras.
  • Hosted the Ingenuity helicopter, the first powered aircraft on another planet.

Curiosity Rover

Investigating habitability in Gale Crater.

Mission Goals

  • Assess whether Gale Crater ever offered environmental conditions favorable for life.
  • Study Martian climate and geology in detail.
  • Serve as a pathfinder for future robotic and human missions.

Launch and Landing

Curiosity launched in November 2011 and landed in August 2012 using the first sky-crane landing on Mars, touching down inside Gale Crater near Mount Sharp.

Key Technical Features

  • Car-sized rover with a plutonium-powered radioisotope generator.
  • Robotic arm with drill and scoop for collecting rock and soil.
  • Laboratory instruments that can analyze samples on board.
  • Multiple cameras for panoramic, microscopic, and chemical imaging.

Sojourner (Bonus)

The first successful Mars rover.

Part of the Mars Pathfinder mission, Sojourner landed in 1997 and proved that mobile robots could safely operate on the Martian surface. Its small wheeled design paved the way for all later rovers.
