Frequently Asked Questions

This page answers common questions about Mars, NASA's rover missions, and how robotic explorers operate so far from Earth.

Why send rovers to Mars instead of humans?

Rovers can operate in extreme environments without risking human life. They can test landing systems, study hazards like dust and radiation, and collect data needed to plan safe human missions in the future.

How long does it take a rover command to reach Mars?

The one-way travel time for radio signals between Earth and Mars varies with distance, typically from about 5 to more than 20 minutes. Rovers must be able to drive and operate safely with a delay, using onboard software and autonomy.

What powers Curiosity and Perseverance?

Both rovers use radioisotope power systems, which convert heat from the natural decay of plutonium into electricity. This allows them to operate day and night and through dusty seasons when sunlight is limited.

How do rovers look for signs of past life?

Rovers study rocks and sediments that formed in ancient lakes, rivers, or deltas. They use cameras and spectrometers to identify minerals and organic molecules that could have preserved evidence of past microbial life.

Can rovers bring samples back to Earth?

Perseverance is designed to collect and store samples in sealed tubes. Future missions, currently being planned, aim to pick up those tubes and return them to Earth for detailed laboratory analysis.
